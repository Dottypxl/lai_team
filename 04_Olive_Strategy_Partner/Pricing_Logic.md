# Olive – Pricing Logic

## 1. Purpose

This file helps Olive think about pricing.

Pricing should protect the work, the client result, and Kirsi's capacity.

Do not price from guilt.

Do not price from fantasy.

Price from scope, value, effort, proof, risk, and business need.

## 2. Pricing principles

Luopua pricing should be:

- clear
- honest
- connected to scope
- high enough to protect time
- grounded in the client result
- simple enough to explain
- not built on pressure
- not built on shame

## 3. What price must cover

Price must cover:

- preparation
- thinking
- meetings
- writing or making
- revisions
- admin
- follow-up
- emotional load
- expertise
- taxes
- tools
- unpaid business time
- recovery time

If the price covers only the visible meeting, it is too low.

## 4. Price floor questions

Before suggesting a price, ask:

- How many hours will this really take?
- How much thinking happens before the client sees anything?
- How many decisions does this require?
- How many revisions are included?
- What is the energy cost?
- What is the risk of scope creep?
- What would make this worth doing again?

## 5. Pricing formula

Use this simple formula as a thinking tool:

```text
Base time cost
+ thinking value
+ delivery complexity
+ revision risk
+ business value
= price range
```

Do not show this formula to clients unless useful.

Use it to avoid underpricing.

## 6. Price levels

### Entry offer

Purpose:

Small paid start.

Examples:

- clarity check
- website review
- one-session clarity work
- one AI helper setup

Pricing logic:

Should be accessible but not cheap.

Must cover prep, session, and written output.

### Core offer

Purpose:

Main paid work.

Examples:

- brand clarity project
- website clarity project
- AI helper system for one business area

Pricing logic:

Should reflect the full thinking and delivery load.

### Project offer

Purpose:

Larger scoped build.

Examples:

- website package
- brand base plus website copy
- AI team setup
- content system build

Pricing logic:

Needs clear phases, limits, and payment terms.

### Care offer

Purpose:

Light support after a core project.

Examples:

- monthly review
- content prompts
- AI helper refinement
- website copy updates

Pricing logic:

Must stay light.

If it becomes ongoing production, reprice or split.

## 8. Pricing for clarity session

Use this thinking:

The clarity session is not only the meeting.

It includes:

- reading messy material
- finding the red thread
- session time
- written clarity base
- next-step recommendation

Price should not be set as “one hour of talking.”

It is a focused business clarity product.

Before final price, define:

- session length
- prep time
- written output length
- revision or no revision
- delivery time
- support after delivery

## 9. Pricing for AI helper setup

One AI helper setup should include:

- role instructions
- voice boundaries
- one workflow file
- source file list
- usage notes

Price depends on:

- how much brand material exists
- how much source material Kirsi must create
- whether it is one helper or a system
- whether testing is included
- whether handover support is included

Boundary:

Do not sell a full AI team at one-helper pricing.

## 10. Pricing for community work

Community work can be:

- paid
- partly paid
- volunteer
- funded
- barter
- proof-building

But it must be chosen clearly.

Ask:

- Why are we doing this?
- Who benefits?
- What is the time limit?
- What is the budget?
- What paid work does this displace?
- What proof or relationship does this create?
- What is the stop point?

If there is no price, there must be a boundary.

## 11. Discount rules

Discount only when there is a clear reason.

Good reasons:

- pilot client
- strong case study
- community choice
- barter with real value
- reduced scope
- early test with clear feedback

Bad reasons:

- guilt
- fear they will say no
- because the client is small
- because Kirsi wants to be nice
- because the work feels easy
- because AI was used

AI can reduce production time.

It does not remove judgement, method, or responsibility.

## 12. Scope creep price triggers

Reprice or pause when:

- new pages are added
- new audience groups are added
- new decision-makers join
- extra meetings are requested
- revisions exceed scope
- client source material is missing
- project direction changes
- delivery becomes urgent
- support becomes ongoing

Use this line:

This is outside the agreed scope. I can price it as an added piece or we can keep it for a later phase.

## 13. Payment structure

For larger projects, use phased payment.

Examples:

```text
50% to book
50% before final delivery
```

or

```text
40% to book
40% after first draft or midpoint
20% before final delivery
```

For small sessions:

Payment before booking or before delivery.

## 15. Price language for clients

Use clear language.

Good:

The price for this session is [price]. It includes prep, the session, and a written clarity base.

Good:

This project starts at [price]. Final pricing depends on the number of pages, source material, and whether copywriting is included.

Good:

That is outside the agreed scope. I can price it as an added piece if you want to include it.

Avoid:

- It is just a small thing.
- I can do that quickly.
- No worries, I will add it.
- I used AI, so it is cheaper.
- Pay what you can unless that is the real offer.

## 16. When price feels uncomfortable

Ask:

- Is the scope clear?
- Is the result clear?
- Is the buyer clear?
- Is the price tied to the real work?
- Is the discomfort about value, fear, or fit?
- Would I want to do this again at this price?

If you would resent doing it again at this price, raise the price or cut the scope.

## 17. Final rule

The price should make the work possible.

If the price makes the work draining, it is not kind.

Not to Kirsi.

Not to the client.

Not to the business.
