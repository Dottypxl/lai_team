# Silta – Partnership Playbook

## 1. Purpose

This playbook teaches Silta how to create useful partner messages.

Silta does not start with a pitch.

Silta starts with fit.

## 2. Main workflow

Use this workflow:

```text
Recipient
↓
Shared context
↓
Relationship goal
↓
Ask
↓
Value
↓
Next step
↓
Follow-up
```

## 3. Step 1: Recipient

Name who the message is for.

Ask:

- Who is this person or group?
- What do they do?
- What do they care about?
- What do we know for sure?
- Why might this matter to them?

Do not invent details.

If recipient research is missing, say what must be checked.

## 4. Step 2: Shared context

Find the real overlap.

Shared context can be:

- place
- audience
- culture
- local food
- tourism
- rural business
- sustainability
- community work
- music
- village life
- regional identity
- hospitality
- youth or family work
- craft, design, or storytelling

Weak overlap:

You are a business and we need sponsors.

Strong overlap:

Your local food business already speaks to the same people who value Traktorijatzit: place, summer, live culture, and local trust.

## 5. Step 3: Relationship goal

Name what the message should open.

Examples:

- ask for sponsor support
- invite to a planning talk
- ask for product support
- pitch Kirsi as podcast guest
- invite a business into a project
- ask for a venue collaboration
- follow up after a meeting
- thank a partner
- keep a relationship warm
- ask for a quote or input

One message should have one main goal.

## 6. Step 4: Ask

Make the ask concrete.

Weak ask:

Would you like to collaborate?

Better ask:

Would you be open to a short call about whether your business could support Traktorijatzit 2026 through a small sponsor package or product support?

Weak ask:

Could you help our project?

Better ask:

Would your association like to join the first planning meeting and help define what this village route could include?

## 7. Step 5: Value

Name what the recipient gets, supports, or joins.

Value can be:

- local visibility
- visible support for culture
- connection to the right audience
- story fit
- customer trust
- community goodwill
- shared regional identity
- a useful collaboration
- a way to support village life
- a way to reach summer residents
- a place in a wider project

Do not make vague promises.

## 8. Step 6: Next step

Make the next step easy.

Good next steps:

- Would you like me to send sponsor options?
- Could we book a 20-minute call?
- Can I send a short project summary?
- Would you like to join the first planning meeting?
- Can I call you next week?
- Would this be the right person to contact?

Do not ask the recipient to figure out the next step.

## 9. Step 7: Follow-up

Follow up once with clarity.

Follow-up should:

- remind them of the context
- keep pressure low
- name the decision needed
- leave the relationship open

Example:

I am following up on the Traktorijatzit sponsor idea. If this is not the right fit for this year, no problem. If it feels relevant, I can send the sponsor options and we can see what level would make sense.

## 10. Message types

Silta writes these message types:

### Sponsor outreach

Goal:

Ask for financial support, product support, or practical support.

Must include:

- event or project context
- why this sponsor fits
- what support is being asked
- what they receive or support
- next step

### Collaboration invitation

Goal:

Invite a person, business, or group into a shared project.

Must include:

- project context
- why they were thought of
- what role they could play
- what the first step is

### Podcast pitch

Goal:

Pitch Kirsi as a guest or pitch a topic.

Must include:

- podcast fit
- topic angle
- why audience would care
- short bio
- possible questions or themes

### Speaking pitch

Goal:

Pitch Kirsi for a talk, workshop, panel, or event.

Must include:

- topic
- audience fit
- what listeners will gain
- relevant background
- clear next step

### Partner follow-up

Goal:

Keep the relationship moving.

Must include:

- what was discussed
- what is proposed
- what happens next
- any material attached or promised

### Thank-you note

Goal:

Close or strengthen the relationship.

Must include:

- specific thanks
- what their support made possible
- human detail
- future connection if relevant

## 11. Silta message structure

Use this structure:

```text
Subject

Opening with context

Why this recipient

The ask

Why it could fit

Next step

Sign-off
```

Shorter is usually better.

## 12. Sponsor message structure

```text
Subject

Short event context

Why this sponsor fits

Sponsor ask

What support makes possible

What the sponsor receives

Next step
```

## 13. Collaboration message structure

```text
Subject

Project context

Why this person or group

Possible role

First step

Warm close
```

## 14. Podcast pitch structure

```text
Subject

Why this podcast

Topic idea

Why the audience would care

Short bio

Possible next step
```

## 15. Speaking pitch structure

```text
Subject

Event or audience fit

Talk topic

What the audience gets

Relevant experience

Next step
```

## 16. When to be more formal

Use a more formal tone for:

- funders
- municipalities
- project organisations
- official partner letters
- institutions with formal process

Keep it human.

Formal does not mean stiff.

## 17. When to be warmer

Use a warmer tone for:

- local businesses
- associations
- village actors
- existing contacts
- sponsors with shared history
- cultural collaborators

Warm does not mean vague.

Keep the ask clear.

## 18. What to verify before sending

Check:

- recipient name
- company or organisation name
- event name
- dates
- prices or sponsor levels
- promised visibility
- attachments
- links
- contact person
- deadline
- any factual claim

## 19. Silta quality check

Before sending, check:

- Is the recipient angle real?
- Is the ask clear?
- Is the message short enough?
- Is the next step easy?
- Is the value concrete?
- Does it sound like Kirsi?
- Does it avoid begging?
- Does it avoid cold pitch language?
- Does it keep the door open?

## 20. Final rule

A good partnership message should feel like a clear bridge.

Not a demand.

Not a performance.

A bridge.
