Luopua Lähde — Beta Build Decision

  Acting as Verso. Working principle: Decide → Cut → Specify → Hand off.

  ---
  1. Approved Beta Shape

  This is the beta. Nothing more.

  Pre-work — async exercise sent after booking, returned before the session.
  Session — 90 minutes. One thread anchor, one excavation, one document map.
  Delivery — Lähde source document. Sent within 72 hours.
  Follow-up — One feedback message. Three questions. Built into the delivery email.

  Price: 175 € beta price, clearly named as beta.
  Clients: 3. Not 5. Three surfaces patterns without overextending before the offer is
  refined.
  Format: Video call. No in-person requirement for beta.
  Language: Finnish-first for beta. This is the core audience. English can come later.

  This shape is approved. It tests the core promise — the material already exists; Lähde
  finds it and gives it a home — in the smallest version that can deliver a real result.

  ---
  2. What Must Be Cut

  Cut now. Not deferred. Removed from beta entirely.

  Cut: any content planning layer.
  Lähde is a source document. The moment it becomes a content calendar or posting plan, it
  is a different product. If a client asks: that is a future offer. Name it in the session
  boundary and move on.

  Cut: AI helper setup or prompt writing.
  The document tells clients how to feed the material into AI. Building the AI system for
  them is AI Helper Setup — a separate service. Not included here. Not mentioned as a
  deliverable.

  Cut: designed document template.
  Plain text document for beta. Google Doc, clean, no Luopua visual design applied yet. The
   structure is still forming. Design freezes what should still be flexible.

  Cut: any second session or follow-up call.
  90 minutes is the container. The delivery document is the product. If something important
   didn't fit, name it in the document as outside scope with a suggested next step. Do not
  offer to continue informally.

  Cut: a public offer page.
  A personal invitation is enough for 3 beta clients. The offer page gets written after the
   beta, from real client words. Writing it now means writing it in the wrong language.

  Cut: a Selkeytys-Lähde bundle.
  The pairing is logical. It is not ready. Both offers must prove themselves individually
  before being packaged together. Observe which clients need which, in which order. Let the
   bundle shape come from real use.

  Cut: social warm-up content series.
  Sointu builds this — but not yet. Warm-up content is for a public launch. The beta is
  three personal invitations. Content comes after the offer is refined.

  3. Exact Deliverables

  What the client receives:

  One document. Named: Lähde — [Client name] — [Date]

  Seven sections. Each with a defined job. No section is decoration.

  ---
  Thread anchor
  One sentence. What this business is here to say.
  Written during the session, confirmed by the client before it goes in the document. If it
   shifted during excavation, the final version goes here. Not invented — found.

  Stories
  Two to three short paragraphs.
  Real moments from the work. Written close to how the client told them. Not polished.
  These carry weight because they are specific and true. Used for: content, website
  sections, AI source material.

  Client language
  Eight to twelve phrases.
  Exact words or close paraphrase from how clients talk about the work, what they ask for,
  what they say they got. These are overheard language, not invented copy. Used for:
  website copy, offer descriptions, social captions, AI input.

  What this business believes
  Three to five statements.
  Not values. Not mission. Specific things this business knows from doing the work that are
   not obvious to the outside. The knowledge that comes from years of practice. Used for:
  content angles, offer differentiation, newsletter voice.

  Place and rhythm
  One short paragraph.
  The physical, seasonal, or contextual reality of this work. What shapes the pace, the
  client relationship, the offer. For rural and place-based businesses, this is often their
   most distinctive and most unused material.

  Recurring questions
  Three to five questions.
  What clients ask before buying, during the work, or after. Answered verbally for years.
  Never written down. Used for: website FAQ, pre-purchase content, email responses.

  How to use this document
  Three sentences maximum.
  One instruction for where to start. One sentence on AI use. One sentence on when to
  return to it.

  ---
  What the client does not receive:
  Content calendar. Strategy deck. Visual identity. Website copy. AI prompts. A second
  session. Anything not listed above.

  ---
  4. Beta Boundaries

  These are said explicitly — in the invitation, in the session opening, and in the
  delivery message.

  What this is:
  A 90-minute working session that excavates existing material and turns it into a source
  document. One document. One result.

  What this is not:
  A content strategy. A brand project. A full website review. A writing service. Ongoing
  support. A replacement for Selkeytys if the client's real problem is not knowing what
  their business is saying.

  If a client's problem is upstream of content:
  They may need Selkeytys before Lähde. The thread anchor step in the session opening
  should catch this. If 10 minutes in it becomes clear the client doesn't know what they're
   saying yet — not just struggling to write it — name it directly. "Before we excavate
  material, we may need to find the thread. That is a different session. Let me name what
  I'm seeing." Then decide together how to proceed. Do not push through Lähde with a client
   who needs Selkeytys first.

  Scope creep boundary for Kirsi:
  If the session goes deep into website strategy, offer redesign, or business model
  questions — name it. "That belongs in a different conversation. I'm noting it here and we
   can talk about next steps after this session." Do not give free Selkeytys or Olive work
  inside a Lähde session.

  6. Which AI Team Helper Creates Each Asset

  Silta — Beta invitation message (#1)
  Writing one personal message per client, fit to a real relationship. Silta's job is one
  person, one real reason to reach out, one clear ask. Silta does not send the same message
   to all three. Each invitation is written to the specific person. Silta needs: client
  name, brief context on the relationship, and the beta offer description.

  Kutsu — Pre-work exercise (#2)
  The pre-work is a piece of writing that explains the task and invites honest rough
  answers. It needs to be clear without being intimidating. It must sound like Luopua, not
  a questionnaire. Kutsu writes copy that helps the client understand what they're being
  asked to do and why their rough version is exactly what's wanted. Kutsu needs: the list
  of pre-work prompts and the tone frame (reassuring, plain, not academic).

  Verso — Session opening script and excavation question bank (#3, #4)
  These are internal working tools for Kirsi, not client-facing copy. Verso defines the
  10-minute thread anchor step, the transition into excavation, and the bank of questions
  organised by material type (stories, client language, place, belief, recurring
  questions). Verso also flags the decision point — what to do if the client needs
  Selkeytys instead.

  Verso — Delivery document template (#5)
  Plain text template with section headers, brief instruction per section for Kirsi, and a
  reminder of what belongs in each section and what doesn't. Verso owns this because it is
  a delivery structure decision, not a copy decision.

  Aava — Delivery + feedback message (#6) and proof permission ask (#7)
  The message that arrives with the document is client care. It must be warm, short, and
  not make the client feel like they need to do more work before the document makes sense.
  Aava also writes the three feedback questions and the proof permission sentence — these
  are relationship management, Aava's domain. Aava needs: the beta boundary (what the
  document is and isn't), and the three feedback questions to ask.

  Verso — Beta case note template (#8)
  Internal. What Kirsi fills in after each session. Verso designs the structure from the
  Beta Testing Playbook, adapted to Lähde specifically.

  7. Proof to Collect

  The beta must answer five questions. Everything else is secondary.

  1. Does the pre-work produce usable material?
  Track: arrive at the session with something to excavate, or arrive empty. If empty: which
   question failed? Was it too broad, too intimidating, too abstract?

  2. Is 90 minutes the right container?
  Track: what got rushed or cut. What ran long. Whether the thread anchor step takes time
  away from excavation. Whether the document mapping step at the end feels forced.

  3. Does the document feel useful or like homework?
  Ask directly in feedback question 1. Watch language: "I've already used it" vs "I'm not
  sure where to start."

  4. Which section gets used first?
  Ask in the 2–3 week follow-up. This tells us which section is the actual front door of
  the document — which should inform future session emphasis and document section order.

  5. What does the client say they would want next?
  Ask as the last feedback question. This is the next-offer signal. If three clients
  independently name the same next step, that is the next product to build.

  Language to save from each session:
  - The exact phrase the client used to describe their problem before the session
  - The exact phrase the client used when they saw the thread anchor sentence
  - One moment in the excavation where something surfaced that surprised them
  - One direct quote from feedback, with permission noted
