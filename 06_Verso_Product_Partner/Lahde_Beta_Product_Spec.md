# Luopua Lähde Beta Product Specification

## 1. Verdict

Replace the 90-minute live session with the async-first model.

The 90-minute session was built around the method: live excavation, facilitated discovery, and the client as an active participant.

That format was designed from what Kirsi does.

The async-first model is designed from what the client needs to give up to get the result.

The client sends material.
Kirsi does the work.
There is a short call to confirm and clarify.
The client receives a document.

This is a more honest shape for this audience.

Rural small business owners, solo operators, and cultural project people are not necessarily short on commitment. They are short on time and bandwidth for another obligation that feels like homework.

The previous model asked them to show up prepared and participate actively for 90 minutes.

This model asks them to send what they have and show up for 30 minutes.

The offer now feels like relief.

That is the right feeling for this product.

### Main risk

If the material drop is very thin, the 30-minute call carries more weight.

This is a beta learning point.

It is not a reason to add a longer call by default.

### Decision

Async-first version approved.

90-minute live session retired from beta.

## 2. Product thesis

Luopua Lähde is an async-first source excavation service for small businesses that have real material in their work but no single place where it lives.

The client sends what they have: rough, scattered, unpolished.

Kirsi reads it, finds the patterns, and builds a compact source document:

* stories
* client language
* what the business believes
* place and rhythm
* repeated questions
* a thread anchor
* one clear instruction for how to use the source first

The result is a Lähde Source Document.

It is one plain document the client returns to every time they write, brief an AI tool, update a website section, or describe what they do.

The work happens behind the scenes.

The client receives a source, not another task.

### Thesis in one sentence

The material already exists. Lähde finds it, names it, and gives it a home.

### Working promise

You send the scattered material. I find the source you can create from.

## 3. Buyer

### Lähde is for

A small business owner or solo operator with at least two or three years of real work behind them.

They have material:

* stories
* client interactions
* repeated questions
* things they know from doing the work
* text fragments
* old posts
* website copy
* voice notes
* words they use in conversation but never write down

But no single place where that material lives.

Content feels harder than it should.

They start from nothing every time they write.

AI produces generic output because there is nothing solid to feed it.

They can describe what they do in conversation, but it never makes it onto the page.

### Specific buyer signal

They feel like they should be able to do content themselves.

That feeling makes the problem heavier.

They do not need another workshop or a system to manage.

They need someone to do the sorting work for them and hand them something they can actually use.

### Signal phrases

Good-fit buyers may say:

* I know my work is good, but I cannot explain it.
* When I write about it, it comes out wrong.
* I have stories, but I never use them.
* AI sounds like everyone else.
* I always start from zero.
* I have material everywhere, but not in one place.
* I know what I mean when I speak, but not when I write.
* Content feels like wearing someone else’s clothes.

### Lähde is not for

Lähde is not for:

* a business that is just starting out and has little accumulated material
* someone whose primary problem is not knowing what their business is here to say
* someone looking for a content calendar
* someone looking for a social media plan
* someone looking for ongoing content service
* someone who wants to be in the room while all the work happens
* someone who expects AI tool setup
* someone who expects a full content strategy
* someone who wants ghostwriting

### Selkeytys boundary

If the person has no clear accumulated material, or their primary issue is that they do not know what the business is here to say, they may need Selkeytys first.

Lähde finds the source from existing material.

Selkeytys helps when there is not yet enough direction to know what the material should carry.

## 4. Offer shape

The full beta flow:

```text
Material drop
↓
Async source excavation
↓
30-minute clarification call
↓
Lähde Source Document delivery
↓
Feedback and proof collection
```

---

### Step 1: Material drop

After booking, the client receives a short instruction email.

They have 48–72 hours to send their material.

The instruction is brief.

The bar is low.

The client does not need to prepare polished material.

They send what already exists.

---

### Step 2: Async source excavation

Kirsi reads the material.

Kirsi uses the pre-session AI prompt to support sorting.

Kirsi identifies:

* repeated words
* repeated tensions
* possible thread anchor
* stories
* client language
* useful raw material
* gaps
* what needs to be confirmed in the call

Kirsi prepares a preliminary Lähde document structure.

This happens behind the scenes.

The client does nothing during this phase.

---

### Step 3: 30-minute clarification call

This is not an excavation session.

It is a confirmation and gap-filling call.

Kirsi arrives with:

* a thread anchor hypothesis
* early observations
* a draft structure
* three to four things to confirm or collect

The call is used to confirm what she found, collect what was missing, and give the client a brief preview of what the document will hold.

---

### Step 4: Lähde Source Document delivery

Kirsi finalises the document after the call.

Delivery timing:

```text
within 48 hours of the call
```

Delivery format:

```text
plain Google Doc or clean text document
```

No designed template in beta.

The delivery email includes:

* the document
* one instruction for where to start
* three feedback questions

---

### Step 5: Feedback and proof collection

Feedback collection includes:

* three questions with the delivery
* one follow-up question two to three weeks later
* proof permission asked verbally at the end of the clarification call

Proof permission should be asked in the call, not later in an email.

## 5. Client effort

The material drop must be very light.

This is where clients drop out if the bar is too high.

### What the client sends

A short intake plus links or files.

This should take:

```text
10–15 minutes
```

### Intake question 1

What feels most unclear or scattered about how you talk about your work right now?

A few sentences are enough.

No need to be complete.

### Intake question 2

What do you wish people understood about your work that they usually do not?

A few sentences are enough.

Rough is fine.

### Intake question 3

Send one thing you have written about your work that does not quite sound like you.

This can be:

* a website sentence
* a caption
* a bio
* an offer description
* an email
* a screenshot
* a copied text fragment

### Additional material they may send

The client may also send:

* website URL
* Instagram or other social profile URL
* 2–5 minute voice note
* existing text about their work
* brochure text
* old bio
* email to a client
* description they use in person
* old posts
* screenshots of notes
* customer questions

### What they do not need to send

They do not need to send:

* strategy documents
* brand materials
* previous content plans
* polished copy
* anything complete
* anything designed

The rough version is the useful one.

### Material drop reassurance

Use this principle in the client instruction:

Scattered, messy, and partial material is exactly what works best.

The client does not need to prepare.

They send what they have.

## 6. Kirsi’s work

### Before the call

Approximate time:

```text
2–3 hours
```

Kirsi:

1. Reads all submitted material once without notes.
2. Runs the pre-session AI prompt on the submitted material.
3. Reviews the AI output.
4. Identifies what landed and what to discard.
5. Drafts the preliminary Lähde document structure.
6. Notes which sections are strong.
7. Notes which sections have gaps.
8. Writes a tentative thread anchor hypothesis.
9. Notes three to four specific things to confirm or collect in the 30-minute call.
10. Prepares the call opening: what she found and what she is uncertain about.

### After the call

Approximate time:

```text
1.5–2 hours
```

Kirsi:

1. Runs the post-call drafting AI prompt on the call notes.
2. Reviews and edits all flagged sections.
3. Writes the section “How to use this source first.”
4. Removes all guidance brackets from the template.
5. Checks that each section is traceable to what the client said or sent.
6. Reviews whether every section sounds like the client, not like generic copy.
7. Sends the delivery email with the document and feedback questions.

### Total Kirsi time per client

Approximate time:

```text
4–5 hours
```

This work is not concentrated in one block.

## 7. 30-minute clarification call structure

This is a confirmation and clarification call.

Not a facilitated session.

Not a workshop.

Kirsi arrives having done the work.

The call is for what async cannot resolve.

---

### 0–5 min: What I found

Kirsi opens by naming what she found in the material.

This should not be a full summary.

It should be the two or three things that stood out.

Kirsi tells the client what she thinks the thread might be.

Example opening:

```text
I read everything you sent.

The thing that kept coming up was [observation in their words].

I think the thread might be [one sentence].

Does that land?
```

The client does not need to have prepared anything.

They listen and respond.

---

### 5–20 min: Confirm, clarify, collect

Work through the three to four specific gaps or uncertainties from the pre-work.

These are the things async could not resolve.

Typical questions:

* You mentioned [situation] briefly. Can you tell me a bit more about what actually happened?
* What do customers say when they thank you, or when they explain you to someone else?
* What would you want someone to know before they contact you?
* Is there a type of client or project you find yourself turning down? What is it usually?
* What is something you always explain verbally but have never written down?
* Which part of this feels most like your actual work?

Stop at 20 minutes.

Do not let the call expand into full excavation.

If more material exists, it can go into a follow-up note.

It does not need to go into this call.

---

### 20–25 min: Brief preview

Tell the client what the document will hold.

Use one sentence per section.

This manages expectations and gives the client a chance to flag anything that does not belong before it is in the document.

Example:

```text
The document will have two stories, your client language as I found it in the material, and three things your business seems to believe from experience.

Does any of that feel off?
```

---

### 25–30 min: Scope, next step, and proof permission

Name what the document is not.

Use one sentence:

```text
This is not a content calendar, strategy deck, or writing service.
```

Confirm delivery timing:

```text
within 48 hours
```

Ask proof permission verbally:

```text
If you are happy with how this lands, may I use an anonymous note from our work together?

I can remove identifying details or keep it fully anonymous.
```

End on time.

## 8. Lähde Source Document structure

Document name:

```text
Lähde — [Client name] — [Date]
```

Format:

```text
plain Google Doc or clean text document
```

No designed PDF in beta.

No content calendar.

No strategy deck.

---

### 1. Thread anchor

One sentence.

The point running underneath the work.

Confirmed in the call.

Plain language.

Specific to this business.

This is not a slogan.

It is a working anchor.

---

### 2. Stories

Two to three short paragraphs.

Real moments from the work.

Written close to how the client described them.

Specific details kept in.

Not polished into case studies.

Useful for:

* content
* website copy
* AI source material
* offer descriptions
* About page material

---

### 3. Client language

Eight to twelve phrases.

Exact words or close paraphrases.

What clients say.

Not what the business wishes they would say.

Useful for:

* website copy
* offer descriptions
* captions
* FAQs
* emails
* AI prompts

---

### 4. What this business believes

Three to five statements.

Specific knowledge from doing this work.

Not values.

Not mission.

Not generic claims.

These should be things the business knows from experience that are not obvious to the outside.

Useful for:

* content angles
* newsletter voice
* offer differentiation
* homepage sections
* about page

---

### 5. Place and rhythm

One paragraph.

The physical, seasonal, or contextual reality of the work.

What shapes the pace, the client, the offer, or the way the work is done.

For rural and place-based businesses, this is often distinctive and underused material.

Useful for:

* About page
* captions
* place-based content
* service descriptions
* brand voice

---

### 6. What gets asked again and again

Three to five recurring questions.

Written as people actually ask them.

With brief honest answers.

Useful for:

* website FAQ
* email replies
* content
* sales conversations
* pre-purchase clarity

---

### 7. How to use this source first

Three sentences.

Written by Kirsi.

Not drafted by AI.

This section includes:

1. One instruction specific to this client’s situation.
2. One sentence on AI use.
3. One sentence on when to return to the document.

Example structure:

```text
Start with [specific section] before writing anything new.

If you use AI, give it [specific source section] first so it does not start from generic advice.

Return to this document whenever content starts to feel borrowed or unclear.
```

## 9. AI-assisted workflow

AI helps Kirsi sort.

AI does not decide what matters.

### Where AI helps

AI can help with:

* reading submitted material
* identifying repeated words, phrases, and patterns
* flagging likely surface problem versus possible deeper problem
* proposing a tentative thread anchor for Kirsi to test
* identifying material that could become stories
* extracting client language phrases from submitted text
* noting gaps
* suggesting session questions
* drafting document sections from call notes
* flagging invented or uncertain content within a draft

### Where Kirsi stays the final judge

Kirsi stays the final judge for:

* confirming the thread anchor
* deciding which stories go in the document
* deciding which stories stay out
* editing client language so it reflects what the client actually said
* evaluating whether beliefs are specific enough or still generic
* writing “How to use this source first”
* the final read before the document is sent

### What must not be automated

Do not automate:

* the thread anchor decision
* client language invention
* the delivery of the document
* the proof permission conversation
* the “How to use this source first” section
* final judgement about what matters

### Traceability rule

Any section AI drafts must be traceable back to something the client actually said or sent.

If it cannot be traced, it does not go in the document.

## 10. Beta price

### Beta price

```text
165 €
```

### Later public price

```text
265–295 €
```

### Pricing reasoning

The async model is a concierge-style service.

The client sends material and receives a finished document.

They attend one short call.

This is a higher-value experience for the client than a 90-minute workshop where they do significant work themselves.

The client effort is low.

Kirsi’s effort is still significant: approximately 4–5 hours of skilled reading, sorting, judgement, writing, and delivery.

The beta price acknowledges:

* the process is still being tested
* the work is real
* the document is a usable working tool
* the audience may be price-sensitive
* the offer should not be positioned as cheap content help

### Beta framing for the client

Use this wording:

```text
This is a beta price.

I am testing the process with a small number of people before opening it publicly.

The work is real.

In return, I will ask for honest feedback.
```

### Pricing watchpoint

During beta, track:

* Did anyone hesitate at the price?
* Did anyone say it felt too cheap?
* Did the delivery time feel sustainable?
* Did the work take more than 5 hours?
* Did the client understand what they were buying?

If the work consistently takes more than 5 hours, the public price must rise.

## 12. Proof to collect

The beta must answer these six questions.

Everything else is secondary.

### 1. Was the material drop easy enough?

Track:

* Did clients complete the intake?
* Did they send material without friction?
* Did anyone drop out?
* Did anyone send nothing?
* Did the instruction feel clear?

This tests whether client effort is calibrated correctly.

---

### 2. Did Kirsi find enough material async?

Track:

* Could the pre-call AI-assisted prep produce a credible structure?
* Was the submitted material rich enough?
* Did the call need to do most of the excavation?
* Which submitted material was most useful?

This tests the feasibility of the async model.

---

### 3. Was 30 minutes the right call length?

Track:

* Did the call confirm and clarify?
* Did it need to excavate from scratch?
* Did it feel rushed?
* Did it stay inside scope?
* Did the client feel heard?

This tests whether async prep is doing its job.

---

### 4. Does the document feel like the client’s?

Ask directly in feedback.

Watch for:

```text
Yes, this is exactly it.
```

versus:

```text
It is good, but not quite.
```

The gap tells where Kirsi’s interpretation may be overriding the client’s actual material.

---

### 5. Which section gets used first?

Ask in the two-to-three week follow-up.

This tells which section is the real front door of the document.

It should inform future session focus and product positioning.

---

### 6. What does the client want next?

Ask as the last feedback question.

Three clients independently naming the same next step is a signal for the next offer to build.

---

### Language to capture from each client

Capture:

* the phrase they used to describe their problem before sending material
* the phrase they used when they saw the thread anchor sentence in the call
* the exact words from feedback, with permission noted
* what they said the document gave them
* what they said they used first
* what they asked for next

## 17. First beta test plan

Use three beta clients.

Different buyer types.

Each one teaches something different.

### Client 1: Content-frozen solo operator

Who:

A small business owner in Kirsi’s existing network who has mentioned not knowing what to write or post.

Why first:

Known trust reduces friction.

They are likely to be honest about what did and did not land.

What to learn:

* Was the material drop easy?
* Did the async prep create enough structure?
* Did the thread anchor land?
* Did 30 minutes hold?

After this session:

Revise material drop instructions and call opening if needed.

---

### Client 2: Place-based or rural operator

Who:

Someone working from a specific place: hospitality, farm, village service, seasonal work, cultural place, or rural setting.

Why second:

Tests whether Lähde works for the core Luopua audience.

Also tests whether the “Place and rhythm” section earns its place.

What to learn:

* Does the document capture place as actual content material?
* Do rural stories surface naturally?
* Does the client see place as usable source material after the session?
* Does the place section feel strong or like filler?

After this session:

Refine place-focused prompts and the Place and rhythm section if needed.

---

### Client 3: AI-frustrated user

Who:

Someone who has tried AI for content and found the output generic, flat, or wrong-sounding.

Why third:

Tests the claim that Lähde solves the AI-source problem.

What to learn:

* Does the document give them useful AI input?
* Is “How to use this source first” specific enough?
* What is the gap between the document and what AI actually needs?
* Is AI Helper Setup a natural next offer?

After this session:

Decide whether Lähde needs a short AI-use note or whether that belongs fully in a separate AI Helper Setup offer.

## 18. Beta success criteria

The beta is successful when:

* three sessions are delivered
* the document structure is revised at least once
* one quote or case note is collected with permission
* at least two clients say the document helped them stop starting from nothing
* the 30-minute call confirms and clarifies instead of becoming a full excavation
* Kirsi can deliver the document without the process becoming too heavy
* one repeated next-offer signal appears

The beta is not successful only because clients were polite.

The beta is successful if it teaches what is true.
