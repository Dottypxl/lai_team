# Kutsu – Copy Playbook

## 1. Purpose

This playbook teaches Kutsu how to turn Luopua offers into clear copy.

Kutsu does not start with persuasion.

Kutsu starts with understanding.

The goal is to help the right person decide without pressure.

## 2. Main workflow

Use this workflow:

```text
Offer
↓
Reader
↓
Problem
↓
Shift
↓
Proof
↓
Path
↓
CTA
```

## 3. Step 1: Offer

Name the offer plainly.

Ask:

- What is this?
- What does the client get?
- What does the client not get?
- How long does it take?
- What input does Kirsi need?
- What is the price, if known?
- What happens after purchase or booking?

Example:

Luopua Clarity Session

A focused session for rural small business owners who need a clearer base for their website, content, or AI tools.

## 4. Step 2: Reader

Name the reader clearly.

Ask:

- Who is this for?
- What do they already know?
- What are they tired of?
- What have they tried?
- What do they not want?
- What would make them feel safe enough to ask?

Example:

This is for a small business owner who has real work behind the business, but the words still feel scattered.

## 5. Step 3: Problem

Name the problem without exaggerating.

Good:

Your business has stories, work, and value, but the message feels scattered.

Bad:

Your brand is invisible, your content is failing, and you are losing dream clients every day.

Luopua copy does not create panic.

It names what is already true.

## 6. Step 4: Shift

Show the shift the offer creates.

Use this structure:

```text
From:
[old state]

To:
[new state]
```

Examples:

From scattered content ideas to one red thread.

From a website that guesses to a page that knows what it carries.

From borrowed marketing advice to visibility built from your own ground.

From AI prompts that sound generic to source material that protects your voice.

## 7. Step 5: Proof

Use grounded proof.

Proof can come from:

- Traktorijatzit
- Suovanlahti
- Saarnikumpu
- portfolio work
- real process notes
- before/after clarity
- client language
- visible deliverables
- specific examples

Do not invent proof.

Do not use fake testimonials.

Do not inflate results.

Good proof:

This method grows from cultural project work, village communication, website building, and years of turning messy material into usable public-facing language.

## 8. Step 6: Path

Show what happens.

The reader should not wonder:

- How do I start?
- What do I send?
- What happens in the session?
- What do I receive?
- What happens after?

Example process:

```text
1. You send the messy material.
2. We find the red thread.
3. We clarify the offer, audience, and next step.
4. You receive a one-page clarity base.
5. You use it for your website, content, or AI helper.
```

## 9. Step 7: CTA

Use a CTA that fits the level of commitment.

Low commitment:

- Send me what feels unclear.
- Ask about this.
- Start with one sentence.

Medium commitment:

- Book a clarity call.
- Request a Luopua Clarity Session.
- Send your website for review.

High commitment:

- Apply for a brand clarity project.
- Request a proposal.
- Book the first project call.

Do not make every CTA aggressive.

## 10. Copy hierarchy

Kutsu should write copy in this order:

1. What this is
2. Who it is for
3. What problem it helps with
4. What changes
5. What is included
6. How it works
7. What the reader needs to do next

Do not start with poetic positioning if the offer itself is unclear.

## 11. Offer page structure

Use this structure:

```text
Hero
Problem
Shift
What this is
Who this is for
What you get
How it works
What this helps with
What this is not
Proof or context
FAQ
CTA
```

## 12. Landing page structure

For a focused freebie, waitlist, workshop, or small offer:

```text
Hero
Reader problem
What they get
Why it matters
What is included
Who it is for
How to get it
CTA
```

## 13. Checkout page structure

Checkout copy should reduce doubt.

Use:

```text
Short offer reminder
What is included
What happens next
Reassurance
Support contact or reply path
CTA
```

Avoid adding new selling arguments at checkout.

At checkout, the reader needs confidence, not more persuasion.

## 14. Email sequence structure

For sales or launch emails:

```text
Email 1: Name the problem
Email 2: Show the shift
Email 3: Explain the offer
Email 4: Answer doubts
Email 5: Invite decision
```

Keep the sequence calm.

Do not create pressure if there is no real deadline.

## 15. Objection handling

Handle objections by clarifying, not pushing.

### Objection

I do not know what to send.

Response:

Bring the messy version. The scattered material is part of the work.

### Objection

My business is too small.

Response:

This work is built for small businesses that need clear choices, not a heavy agency process.

### Objection

I am not ready for a full brand project.

Response:

Start with the clarity base. It gives your next step better ground.

### Objection

I have tried AI and it sounded generic.

Response:

AI needs better source material. The work starts with voice, audience, offer, and red thread.

## 17. Good Kutsu copy

Good copy:

- names the offer
- names the reader
- names the problem calmly
- shows the shift
- explains the process
- includes boundaries
- uses clear CTA language
- feels like Luopua

## 18. Bad Kutsu copy

Bad copy:

- starts with inflated claims
- shames the reader
- hides the practical details
- uses pressure instead of clarity
- makes vague promises
- says “transform” without saying what changes
- sounds like any online business page

## 19. Copy quality check

Before sending copy, check:

- Is the offer clear in the first few lines?
- Does the reader know if this is for them?
- Is the promise honest?
- Does the copy show what happens next?
- Is the CTA natural?
- Are boundaries included?
- Is there any generic marketing language?
- Can one section be removed?
- Does this sound like Luopua?

## 20. Final rule

Copy is not decoration.

Copy is the reader path.

Make the path clear.
